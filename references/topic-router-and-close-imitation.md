# Topic Router and Close Imitation

Use this before selecting reference articles for MS Marketing rewriting or imitation.

## Classification Rule

Classify the manuscript by the firm-side marketing lever first, then by the consumer process, then by the market consequence.

Report:

```text
Primary topic class:
Secondary topic class:
Research object:
Method:
Firm lever:
Consumer process:
Market consequence:
Same-class templates:
What to imitate:
What not to imitate:
```

If a manuscript spans multiple classes, choose the class that owns the paper's research question, not the class that merely supplies examples or citations.

## Method Classification

Always classify the method separately from the topic.

Common MS Marketing methods:

- Analytical game-theory model: firm/consumer strategic interaction, equilibrium, comparative statics.
- Dynamic model: state variables, transitions, dynamic pricing, retention, diffusion, or repeated purchase.
- Search model: consumer search order, search cost, discovery, matching, or journey control.
- Information design / persuasion model: disclosure, observability, belief updating, signal design, or learning.
- Targeting / personalization model: consumer data, segmentation, privacy response, behavior-based action.
- Network / diffusion model: WOM, peer effects, social influence, seeding, adoption, or disadoption.
- Structural / empirical model: field data, estimated demand/retention/network effects, counterfactuals.
- Experiment / behavioral evidence: lab/field experiment, survey, or behavioral measurement.
- Numerical / simulation analysis: used to compare policies when closed-form characterization is limited.

Report whether the method is:

- Same as the strongest comparables.
- Adjacent but acceptable for the topic.
- Potentially weaker because the topic's MS Marketing comparables usually use another method.
- Stronger/different because it gives a cleaner mechanism or broader policy comparison.

## Comparable-Paper Benchmark

Before rewriting, build a compact benchmark against same-topic Management Science Marketing articles.

Use local notes/PDFs first. If the user asks for current or exact department acceptance status, verify from official article pages before making current claims.

For each comparable, extract:

```text
Paper:
Research object:
Method:
Core conclusion:
What it does better than us:
What we do better / can claim differently:
How to imitate it:
What not to import:
```

Use "better" and "worse" only on named dimensions:

- topic proximity
- method fit
- mechanism clarity
- result novelty
- counterintuitive punch
- managerial relevance
- empirical/phenomenon grounding
- proof/result completeness
- writing clarity

Do not say a paper is globally better or worse. Say, for example, "better as an opening template," "weaker on direct topic proximity," or "stronger methodologically but less close to our research object."

## Topic Classes

### 1. Pricing and Intertemporal Demand

Use when the central lever is price, discount timing, price commitment, behavior-based pricing, retention pricing, or dynamic demand management.

Signals:

- Questions ask when to discount, preannounce, price discriminate, retain, acquire, or poach.
- Consumer process is waiting, repeat purchase, switching, or reacting to past purchase history.
- Main results compare pricing regimes or intertemporal policy rules.

Local templates:

- `Limited Time Offer and Consumer Search`
- `Behavior-Based Pricing: Peer-Induced Fairness`
- `The Strength of Weak Commitments`
- `Retention or Acquisition? Behavior-Based Quality Disclosure`

Imitate:

- Timing clarity, benchmark-first ordering, backward induction exposition, and policy-regime comparison.

Do not imitate:

- Heavy social-network or information-design language unless it drives pricing.

### 2. Search, Discovery, and Consumer Journey

Use when the central process is search order, product discovery, browsing, treasure-hunt utility, rewards, or customer journey design.

Signals:

- Questions ask how a firm changes what consumers inspect first, how long they search, or which path they follow.
- Consumer process is search, exploration, discovery, or engagement.
- Main results identify path ordering, search prominence, or journey distortion.

Local templates:

- `The Joy of Shopping: Thrill of the Treasure Hunt`
- `Limited Time Offer and Consumer Search`
- `Searching for Rewards`
- `Designing Information to Engage Customers`

Imitate:

- Concrete practice opening, conversion of experience into a formal primitive, and journey-level managerial diagnostics.

Do not imitate:

- Pure price-discrimination framing if the paper's novelty is search path control.

### 3. Personalization, Targeting, and Data

Use when the central lever is personalized pricing, personalized product offerings, targeting precision, data collection, privacy, or consumer inference about data use.

Signals:

- Questions ask whether better targeting, more data, or personalization improves profit or welfare.
- Consumer process is inference, privacy response, self-selection, or reaction to being targeted.
- Main results compare uniform vs personalized/targeted policies.

Local templates:

- `To Each Their Own: Personalized Product Offerings in Competition`
- `Personalized Pricing in the Presence of Privacy Concerns`
- `The Effects of Personal Data Management on Competition and Welfare`
- `Multidimensional Targeting and Consumer Response` if available in notes.

Imitate:

- Precision-versus-backlash tension, data granularity as a managerial lever, and unintended-consequence framing.

Do not imitate:

- Generic CRM language unless past behavior is the data source being monetized.

### 4. Social Influence, WOM, and Networks

Use when the central mechanism is peer influence, word of mouth, social comparison, network diffusion, seeding, or visibility among consumers.

Signals:

- Questions ask how peer behavior, public outcomes, social visibility, or network structure changes demand.
- Consumer process is imitation, comparison, diffusion, persuasion by peers, or adoption through contacts.
- Main results compare broad exposure, targeted exposure, WOM, or advertising.

Local templates:

- `Too Popular, Too Fast`
- `Dynamic Pricing in Social Networks: The Word-of-Mouth Effect`
- `Product Policy in Markets with Word-of-Mouth Communication`
- `Can Random Friends Seed More Buzz and Adoption?` if available in notes.

Imitate:

- Decomposition of a broad social force into distinct functions, state-variable exposition, and boundary cases where peer effects help or hurt.

Do not imitate:

- Treating social influence as a uniform demand shifter.

### 5. Platform, Marketplace, and Intermediation

Use when the central lever is platform governance, marketplace rules, disintermediation control, matching, commissions, or repeated transaction architecture.

Signals:

- Questions ask how platform rules affect buyers, sellers, bypassing, repeated interactions, or marketplace participation.
- Consumer/seller process is matching, trust, repeated transaction, off-platform migration, or relationship formation.
- Main results compare platform rule regimes.

Local templates:

- `Platform Disintermediation with Repeated Transactions`
- Platform governance papers in the user's folder or named by the user.

Imitate:

- Multi-sided incentive clarity, rule-regime comparison, and clear separation of platform, seller, and consumer actions.

Do not imitate:

- Single-firm pricing language if the platform's rule design is the true decision.

### 6. Digital Goods, Games, and Monetization Design

Use when the setting is games, virtual products, add-ons, subscriptions, loot boxes, blind boxes, probabilistic products, or digital monetization.

Signals:

- Questions ask how to design virtual products, paid add-ons, stochastic rewards, scarcity, rarity, or in-game advantages.
- Consumer process is winning/losing sensitivity, status, collection, repeat play, or response to uncertain rewards.
- Main results compare product design, probability design, access design, or monetization architecture.

Local templates:

- `Pricing and Design of Pay-to-Win Add-Ons`
- `The Joy of Shopping: Thrill of the Treasure Hunt`
- `Searching for Rewards`
- `Loot Box Pricing and Design` or `Designing Loot Boxes` when available.

Imitate:

- How behavioral primitives become design variables, how digital mechanics become managerial levers, and how counterintuitive product-design results are framed.

Do not imitate:

- Mechanism-design language unless the manuscript truly uses private information and incentive compatibility.

### 7. Information Design, Persuasion, and Misinformation

Use when the central lever is information disclosure, persuasion, observability, message design, misinformation, correction, or learning environment.

Signals:

- Questions ask what customers learn, what the firm reveals, how beliefs update, or how truthful/false information changes behavior.
- Consumer process is learning, belief updating, persuasion, skepticism, or correction.
- Main results compare disclosure/message/information regimes.

Local templates:

- `Designing Information to Engage Customers`
- `Dynamic Persuasion and Strategic Search`
- `Strategic Disinformation Generation and Detection`
- `Will the Truth Free Us from Misinformation?`

Imitate:

- Information environment as a firm-side object, belief-to-action chain, and separation of information effect from price/product effect.

Do not imitate:

- Search or targeting templates unless information changes search or targeting as the main mechanism.

### 8. Welfare, Regulation, and Market Design

Use when the central question is consumer protection, welfare, regulation, counterfeit risk, privacy regulation, or market efficiency.

Signals:

- Questions ask whether a marketing practice should be regulated, whether it harms consumers, or when firm and welfare objectives diverge.
- Consumer process may be secondary; the paper's main contribution is policy or welfare.
- Main results compare laissez-faire, regulation, disclosure, platform rule, or restricted selling format.

Local templates:

- `Regulating Probabilistic Selling of Counterfeits`
- `The Effects of Personal Data Management on Competition and Welfare`
- Loot-box welfare/regulation papers when available.

Imitate:

- Welfare wedge framing, policy counterfactuals, and balanced profit-consumer-surplus language.

Do not imitate:

- Manager-only implication language that hides the policy tradeoff.

## Close Imitation Protocol

Use this for "逐字模仿" requests. The goal is close rhetorical imitation, not verbatim copying.

1. Locate same-class and same-method articles locally.
   - Use the user's specified folder first.
   - Prefer marked-up PDFs or Obsidian notes when available.
   - Extract only the needed section, usually abstract, introduction, model opening, proposition lead-in, or contribution paragraph.
   - If same-topic and same-method comparables conflict, prioritize same-topic for positioning and same-method for technical exposition.

2. Build a sentence-role trace.
   - For each source sentence, label its role: phenomenon, example, puzzle, question, primitive, result, boundary, contribution, roadmap, or implication.
   - Note clause order, specificity level, transition type, and whether the sentence names firm action, consumer process, or market outcome.
   - Do not preserve the source sentence's distinctive wording.

3. Map roles to the user's manuscript.
   - Replace the source phenomenon with the user's phenomenon.
   - Replace the source firm lever with the user's firm lever.
   - Replace the source consumer process with the user's consumer process.
   - Replace the source result with the user's actual theorem/result.

4. Draft a close imitation.
   - Match paragraph length, sentence sequence, role order, and density.
   - Use same-level verbs: design, target, disclose, personalize, preannounce, seed, retain, engage, regulate.
   - Keep technical notation out of the first sentence unless the source article also starts technically.

5. Audit the draft.
   - No long source phrases.
   - No imported assumption that the user's model lacks.
   - No change in the user's theorem meaning.
   - Each paragraph still reads as Management Science Marketing, not a literature note.
